# Kannada Content Plan — Audio-First Curriculum

> Comprehensive content design for Forge's Learn Kannada track.
> Based on research into Pimsleur, TPRS, Michel Thomas, Krashen's i+1,
> and frequency-based vocabulary acquisition.

---

## Design Philosophy

Three principles from the research:

1. **Listen before you speak, speak before you read, read before you write.**
   This is how children acquire language and how every successful audio-first
   program (Pimsleur, Michel Thomas, Language Transfer) is structured.

2. **Words in stories, not in lists.**
   TPRS research shows vocabulary learned in narrative context is retained
   2-3x longer than vocabulary from word lists. Stories give words emotional
   anchors and situational memory.

3. **Active recall, not passive exposure.**
   Pimsleur's graduated interval recall works because the learner PRODUCES
   the word from memory, not just recognizes it. Every listening task should
   demand some form of active response.

---

## Target Vocabulary Scope

Research says 300 well-chosen words = basic survival. 800-1000 = 75% comprehension.

**V1 target: ~350 words (A1 level — survive in Bengaluru)**

| Tier | Words | When | What |
|---|---|---|---|
| Survival core | 50 | Week 1-2 | Greetings, yes/no, numbers 1-10, question words, 5 core verbs, essential nouns |
| Daily life | 100 | Week 3-4 | Food, transport, time, family, more verbs, basic adjectives |
| Expanding | 100 | Week 5-6 | Shopping, medical, weather, colors, directions, feelings |
| Conversational | 100 | Week 7-8 | Activities, plans, opinions, past/future, compound phrases |

This is enough to: order food, take autos, introduce yourself, ask for help,
shop at markets, have basic conversations, understand simple Kannada around you.

## Content Types (8 types across 4 phases)

### Type 1: Situational Micro-Stories
**Method:** Pimsleur + TPRS hybrid
**Format:** 2-3 minute bilingual narration. English frame with Kannada words woven in.
Each story introduces 5-8 new words in a real Bengaluru situation.
Comprehension questions follow.

**Example structure:**
```
[Narration] Ramu walked to the angadi — the small shop near his mane, his house.
[Narration] He said: Namaskara! Ondu bisi kaapi kodi — one hot coffee please.
[Narration] The shopkeeper asked: Sakkare beku? Do you want sugar?
[Question] What does 'angadi' mean? → shop
[Question] What did Ramu order? → one hot coffee
```

**Audio:** Kannada phrases via IndicF5. English via browser TTS.
**Count:** 20 stories (10 existing narration tasks + 10 new)

### Type 2: Graduated Recall Drills
**Method:** Pimsleur's core technique
**Format:** Prompt → pause (user recalls) → answer → build up.
Each drill covers one vocabulary tier, building from words to phrases to sentences.

**Example structure:**
```
"Coffee in Kannada is kaapi. Say: kaapi."
[pause]
"One is ondu. How do you say 'one coffee'?"
[pause]
"Ondu kaapi. Give is kodi. How do you say 'give one coffee'?"
[pause]
"Ondu kaapi kodi. Please is dayavittu. Now say 'please give one coffee.'"
[pause]
"Dayavittu ondu kaapi kodi."
```

**Audio:** Full audio — this is a purely audio content type.
**Count:** 20 drills (new content type, doesn't exist yet)

### Type 3: Circling Exercises
**Method:** TPRS circling technique
**Format:** One target structure, asked 6 ways. Massive comprehensible repetition.

**Example structure:**
```
"Ramu wants coffee. Ramuge kaapi beku."
"Does Ramu want coffee? Ramuge kaapi beku-aa? Haudu!"
"Does Ramu want tea? Ramuge chai beku-aa? Illa! Kaapi beku."
"What does Ramu want? Ramuge enu beku? Kaapi!"
"Who wants coffee? Yarige kaapi beku? Ramuge!"
"Does Priya want coffee or tea? Priyage kaapi beku-aa, chai beku-aa?"
```

**Audio:** Full audio — repetition-heavy, designed for ear training.
**Count:** 15 exercises (new content type)

### Type 4: Survival Dialogues
**Method:** Situational practice
**Format:** Two-person conversation in a common situation.
Listen to full dialogue first, then practice one role.

**Situations needed:**
1. Ordering at a darshini (quick restaurant)
2. Taking an auto rickshaw
3. Buying vegetables at the market
4. Asking for directions on the street
5. Introducing yourself to a neighbor
6. At the doctor's office
7. Buying a bus ticket
8. At a clothing shop
9. Phone call — making plans
10. At a temple / cultural site
11. Checking into a hotel
12. At the pharmacy
13. Asking about someone's family
14. Complaining about weather
15. Ordering food delivery

**Audio:** Both roles via IndicF5 (different reference voices for different characters).
**Count:** 15 dialogues (5 existing + 10 new)

### Type 5: Sound Familiarization
**Method:** Silent period / ear training
**Format:** Pure listening exercises. No production required.
Focus on sounds that don't exist in English.

**Exercises:**
1. Kannada vowel sounds (short vs long: a/aa, i/ii, u/uu, e/ee, o/oo)
2. Retroflex consonants (ṭa, ḍa, ṇa — tongue curled back)
3. Aspirated vs unaspirated (ka/kha, ga/gha, ta/tha, da/dha)
4. Kannada rhythm and stress patterns (word-initial stress)
5. Minimal pairs — similar words with different meanings
6. Connected speech — how words blend in natural Kannada
7. Intonation — questions vs statements vs exclamations

**Audio:** IndicF5 for all examples.
**Count:** 7 exercises (new content type)

### Type 6: Shadowing Exercises
**Method:** Bridges listening → speaking
**Format:** Hear a phrase → repeat it simultaneously (shadow).
Starts slow, speeds up. Focuses on rhythm and intonation, not perfection.

**Structure:**
```
[Slow] "Namaskara, hegiddira?" — repeat with me
[Normal] "Namaskara, hegiddira?" — try to keep up
[Fast] "Namaskara, hegiddira?" — match the speed
```

**Audio:** IndicF5 at different speeds (adjust via generation parameters).
**Count:** 10 exercises (new content type, bridges Phase 1→2)

### Type 7: Context Guessing
**Method:** Krashen's i+1 — one unknown word in comprehensible context
**Format:** Hear a sentence where you know everything except one word.
Guess the meaning from context. Answer revealed.

**Example:**
```
"Ramu is very hungry. He goes to the darshini and orders oota."
"What do you think 'oota' means?"
[pause]
"Oota means food/meal."
```

**Audio:** Bilingual — English context, Kannada target word.
**Count:** 10 exercises (new content type)

### Type 8: Vocabulary Review Spirals
**Method:** Spaced repetition within content
**Format:** Quick-fire recall of previously learned words, mixed across tiers.
Appears every 5-7 tasks to reinforce retention.

**Structure:**
```
"Quick review! What's the Kannada word for..."
"Water?" [pause] "Neeru"
"How much?" [pause] "Eshtu"
"Thank you?" [pause] "Dhanyavada"
"Give one coffee?" [pause] "Ondu kaapi kodi"
```

**Audio:** Full audio drill format.
**Count:** 12 review tasks (new, spaced throughout the curriculum)

## Is This Enough?

### Comparison with established programs

| Program | Content volume | Vocabulary | Level reached |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pimsleur Level 1 | 30 lessons × 30 min = 15 hrs | ~500 words | A1-A2 |
| Language Transfer | 50 lessons × 10 min = 8 hrs | ~300 words | A1 |
| Duolingo (Section 1) | ~200 exercises | ~400 words | A1 |
| **Forge Kannada v1** | **265 tasks × 10 min = 44 hrs** | **~350 words** | **A1** |

We have MORE content than Language Transfer and comparable to Pimsleur Level 1.
The depth is appropriate for A1. This is enough for v1.

### What v1 does NOT cover (future expansion)

- A2 vocabulary (words 350-800): workplace, opinions, storytelling, abstract concepts
- Complex grammar: past/future tense conjugation, compound sentences, conditionals
- Regional dialect variations (Bengaluru Kannada vs Mysuru vs North Karnataka)
- Formal vs informal register (when to use neevu vs neenu)
- Kannada literature and poetry
- Advanced listening: news, podcasts, movies

### When to expand

After 10+ users complete the full curriculum and provide feedback on:
- Which tasks were too easy / too hard
- Where they felt stuck
- What situations they encountered that the curriculum didn't prepare them for
- Whether 350 words was enough for their daily needs

## Story Bible (20 Micro-Stories)

Each story follows a recurring cast of characters in Bengaluru.
Characters provide continuity — learners care about what happens to them.

### Characters
- **Ramu** — 28, software engineer, moved to Bengaluru from Delhi. Learning Kannada.
  (The learner's avatar — makes the same mistakes they would)
- **Lakshmi** — 55, Ramu's landlady. Patient, speaks slowly, teaches him phrases.
- **Priya** — 30, Ramu's colleague. Bilingual, switches between Kannada and English.
- **Venkatesh** — 60, auto driver. Speaks fast Kannada, good-natured.
- **Meera** — 25, runs a darshini. Practical, no-nonsense.

### Story Sequence

**Tier 1 Stories (Survival Core — Week 1-2)**
1. Ramu's First Morning — wakes up, goes to Lakshmi's for coffee, learns greetings
2. At Meera's Darshini — orders breakfast, learns food words and numbers
3. The Auto Ride — Venkatesh takes Ramu to work, learns transport phrases
4. Meeting Priya — introductions at office, learns name/where-from/what-do-you-do
5. Lost in Jayanagar — asks for directions, learns place words and question words

**Tier 2 Stories (Daily Life — Week 3-4)**
6. Ramu Calls Home — talks to Amma on phone, learns family and time words
7. Weekend at the Market — buys vegetables with Lakshmi, learns shopping vocabulary
8. Rainy Evening — stuck at home, learns weather and emotion words with Lakshmi
9. Darshini Regulars — Meera teaches Ramu the full menu, learns all food vocabulary
10. The Bus to Mysuru — takes a day trip, learns travel and direction vocabulary

**Tier 3 Stories (Expanding — Week 5-6)**
11. Ramu Gets Sick — visits doctor, learns body parts and medical phrases
12. Shopping for Clothes — at Commercial Street, learns colors and bargaining
13. Lakshmi's Kitchen — cooking lesson, learns kitchen and ingredient words
14. The Festival — Ugadi celebration, learns culture and celebration vocabulary
15. At the Pharmacy — buys medicine, learns health vocabulary

**Tier 4 Stories (Conversational — Week 7-8)**
16. Ramu's Presentation — uses Kannada at work, learns formal/professional phrases
17. Planning a Trip — makes plans with Priya, learns future tense and activities
18. The Neighbor's Wedding — attends a wedding, learns social and ceremony vocabulary
19. Ramu Teaches Hindi — language exchange with Lakshmi, learns comparison phrases
20. One Year Later — Ramu reflects on his journey, full conversation in Kannada

## Audio Production Notes

### IndicF5 Configuration
- **Model:** AI4Bharat IndicF5 (MIT license, local inference)
- **Machine:** Apple M1 Pro, 32GB RAM
- **Reference voice:** PAN_F_HAPPY_00001.wav for female characters (Lakshmi, Priya, Meera)
- **Second voice needed:** Male reference audio for Ramu, Venkatesh (source TBD)
- **Format:** Generate WAV @ 24kHz → convert to MP3 @ 64kbps
- **English segments:** Browser Web Speech API (IndicF5 doesn't support English)

### Per-Story Audio Structure
Each micro-story produces multiple audio segments:
- Kannada narration segments (IndicF5)
- English explanation segments (browser TTS, not pre-generated)
- Comprehension question prompts (browser TTS)

Only Kannada segments are pre-generated. English is runtime.

### Estimated Audio Volume
- 20 stories × ~8 Kannada segments × ~3s avg = ~8 min
- 20 recall drills × ~12 prompts × ~3s avg = ~12 min
- 15 circling exercises × ~10 prompts × ~3s avg = ~7.5 min
- 15 dialogues × ~8 lines × ~3s avg = ~6 min
- 10 shadowing × ~6 phrases × ~3s avg × 3 speeds = ~9 min
- 7 sound exercises × ~15 examples × ~2s avg = ~3.5 min
- Misc (context guessing, reviews) = ~5 min
- **Total: ~51 minutes of Kannada audio**
- **Storage: ~23 MB as MP3 @ 64kbps** (within Vercel's 100 MB limit)

## Success Criteria

After completing the full curriculum, a learner should be able to:

- [ ] Understand simple Kannada spoken at slow-to-normal speed
- [ ] Order food at a darshini without switching to English
- [ ] Take an auto and give directions in Kannada
- [ ] Introduce themselves and have a 2-minute conversation
- [ ] Recognize and read 50+ common Kannada words in script
- [ ] Count to 100 and handle money transactions
- [ ] Ask for help in emergency situations
- [ ] Understand the gist of simple Kannada conversations around them

This is A1 level — "Can understand and use familiar everyday expressions
and very basic phrases aimed at the satisfaction of needs of a concrete type."
