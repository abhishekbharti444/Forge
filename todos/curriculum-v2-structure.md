## The Research Consensus

Every credible source agrees on a sequential learning path:

```
Physical Foundation → First Sounds → Rhythm → Application →
Expanded Vocabulary → Fretboard Knowledge → Theory as Map →
Ear Development → Creative Expression
```

Key findings:
- **Rhythm should be taught from day 1**, alongside chords — not as a separate later topic
- **Theory should come AFTER playing experience** — "wait until the student has questions like 'why do these chords sound good together?'"
- **Intervals are the foundation of all theory** — chord construction, scales, and harmony all build on intervals
- **Theory sequence matters**: Intervals → Chord Construction → Scales → Roman Numerals/Diatonic Harmony
- **Creativity comes last but is the goal** — improvisation and composition require all prior skills

## Mapping Research to Forge's 3-Level System

### Level 1: Foundation — "Learn to make sounds"
*Goal: Pick up the guitar and make music within the first session*

What belongs here:
- Posture, hand position, pick grip, tuning
- Open chord shapes (Em, Am, C, G, D, E, A)
- Basic strumming (downstrokes, D-DU-UDU)
- Basic rhythm (counting beats, foot tapping, metronome)
- First scales (Em pentatonic box 1, Am pentatonic box 1)
- String names, basic fretboard orientation
- First fingerpicking (p-i-m-a on one chord)
- **Simple progressions to play** (Am-F-C-G, Em-C-G-D, A-D-E)

What does NOT belong here:
- ❌ Music theory quizzes (gp-119) — too early, no playing context
- ❌ Interval shapes on fretboard (gp-074) — needs theory foundation first
- ❌ Reference cards about capo transposition (gp-ref-001) — not a beginner need
- ❌ Chord progression ear training (gp-054) — needs more playing experience

### Level 2: Vocabulary — "Expand your toolkit"
*Goal: Play in any key, understand basic theory, develop your ear*

What belongs here:
- Barre chords (F, Bm, E-shape, A-shape)
- Extended chords (7ths, sus, add9, power chords)
- More strumming patterns (16ths, syncopation, shuffle, ghost strums)
- Fingerpicking patterns (Travis picking, arpeggios with chord changes)
- Pentatonic boxes 2-5, blues scale, major scale
- CAGED system basics
- Fretboard note knowledge (all strings)
- **THEORY SEQUENCE (the critical addition):**
  1. Intervals — what they are, how to see them on fretboard
  2. Chord construction — how chords are built from intervals
  3. Scale construction — how scales are built from intervals
  4. Diatonic harmony — the 7 chords in a key (chord families)
  5. Roman numerals — I-IV-V, I-V-vi-IV, ii-V-I
  6. Relative major/minor — Am and C share the same family
- **EAR TRAINING:**
  - Major vs minor by ear
  - Interval recognition
  - Finding melodies by ear
  - Identifying chord changes
- **INTRODUCTORY IMPROVISATION:**
  - First pentatonic improvisation
  - Space and silence
  - Strumming improvisation
  - Riff creation

### Level 3: Creative — "Make your own music"
*Goal: Improvise, compose, and understand why music works*

What belongs here:
- Advanced improvisation (chord tones, blues, modal, dynamics)
- Composition (progressions for moods, melody writing, song structure)
- Transcription by ear
- Reharmonization
- Advanced theory application (modes, circle of fifths, chord substitution)
- Advanced fingerpicking (simultaneous bass+melody, arrangements)
- Advanced technique (bending, vibrato, cross-string legato)
- Expressive playing (dynamics, phrasing)

## Missing Theory Sequence (the biggest gap)

The curriculum needs a clear theory path. Currently theory is scattered:
- Intervals: gp-074 (L1, wrong level), gp-120 (L2), gp-ret-012 (L1)
- Chord construction: gp-127 (L3), gp-050 (L2)
- Scale construction: gp-069 (L2) — only compares major/minor
- Diatonic harmony: gp-ref-002 (L1 reference card) — passive, not active
- Roman numerals: gp-042 (L2), gp-123 (L2 Nashville numbers)
- Circle of fifths: gp-147 (L3)

### New Theory Bridge Tasks Needed

**L2 Theory Sequence (should be done in order):**

1. **Intervals on the fretboard** — "Play a major 3rd, perfect 5th, and octave from any note. These 3 intervals are the building blocks of all chords."

2. **How chords are built** — "A major chord = root + major 3rd + perfect 5th. Find these intervals inside an E chord shape. Now find them in a G chord shape."

3. **How scales are built** — "The major scale follows a pattern: W-W-H-W-W-W-H (whole and half steps). Play C major and verify this pattern."

4. **The chord family** — "Every major scale produces 7 chords: I(maj) ii(min) iii(min) IV(maj) V(maj) vi(min) vii°(dim). Play all 7 chords in the key of C. Now play all 7 in the key of G."

5. **Why progressions work** — "Am-F-C-G all belong to the key of C (vi-IV-I-V). Every chord in this progression uses only notes from the C major scale. That's why they sound good together."

6. **Relative major and minor** — "Am is the vi chord in C major. That means Am and C major share the exact same 7 chords. Am-F-C-G in the key of C is the same as i-VI-III-VII in the key of Am."

7. **Transposition** — "The I-V-vi-IV pattern works in any key. In C: C-G-Am-F. In G: G-D-Em-C. In D: D-A-Bm-G. Same pattern, different key."

8. **Chord substitution basics** — "Dm and F share two notes (F and A). Try replacing F with Dm in Am-F-C-G. The progression still works but feels different."

## Proposed Skill Area Adjustments

Keep the 6 skill areas but clarify what each one means:

| Skill Area | Focus | L1 | L2 | L3 |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| technique | Physical mechanics | Chromatic exercises, posture, picking | String skipping, legato, speed | Bending, vibrato, expression |
| chords | Chord shapes + harmony | Open chords, basic progressions | Barre chords, 7ths, theory sequence | Creative progressions, composition |
| rhythm | Time and groove | Counting, basic strumming | Syncopation, shuffle, time signatures | Groove creation, polyrhythm |
| scales_fretboard | Notes and patterns | String names, pentatonic box 1 | All boxes, CAGED, major/minor scales | Modes, full-neck fluency |
| ear_training | Listening skills | Major vs minor, tuning | Intervals, chord changes, melody by ear | Transcription, harmonization |
| fingerpicking | Right-hand independence | p-i-m-a basics | Travis picking, arpeggios | Arrangements, simultaneous bass+melody |

The theory sequence lives primarily in **chords** (L2) because chord theory is the most immediately useful theory for a guitarist. Scale theory lives in **scales_fretboard** (L2). Ear training theory lives in **ear_training** (L2).

## The Big Picture

```
L1: MAKE SOUNDS          L2: UNDERSTAND MUSIC       L3: CREATE MUSIC
─────────────────        ─────────────────────      ─────────────────
Open chords              Barre chords               Improvisation
Basic strumming          Theory sequence             Composition
Basic rhythm             Ear training                Transcription
First scales             Pentatonic boxes            Advanced expression
Finger exercises         CAGED system                Song structure
Tuning, posture          Fingerpicking patterns      Reharmonization
Simple progressions      Intro improvisation         Modal playing
```

This maps directly to the research:
- L1 = Stages 1-4 (physical → first sounds → rhythm → application)
- L2 = Stages 5-8 (vocabulary → fretboard → theory → ear)
- L3 = Stage 9 (creative expression)
